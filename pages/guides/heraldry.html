To the right are three references on heraldry used by Civil Air Patrol, Florida Wing, and the United States Air Force. These provide guidance on patch design, colors, symbolism, shapes, and proper heraldic structure.

The goal of an emblem is to make an immediate visual impact without relying on text to explain what it represents or who it belongs to. Many emblems, however, tend to overcrowd the design with text or scrolls that attempt to explain the unit, which can detract from the emblem’s effectiveness.

Overcrowded Emblems


It may seem unconventional, but removing the mottos from the updated designs. In many cases, the mottos referenced actions or expectations that our members should already be demonstrating, making them unnecessary for the emblem itself. In cases where numbers as text would normally appear inside the emblem, they were replaced them with stars to promote consistency and incorporated the numbers into the design, but not as text.

Numerals incorporated into the design


Where units did not have a current emblem or the design lacked certain elements, elements were incorporated from older references to emblems to preserve historical continuity and honor tradition. For research and inspiration, emblems were consulted from archival resources such as the National Headquarters Emblem Archives and member-collected patch libraries, accessible here: Emblem Archives Patch Library Color was a major differentiating factor in all designs, although they all used the same basic palette of reds, blues, whites, and yellows. Standardized the colors across all emblems to maintain consistency and align with Official Civil Air Patrol colors should be used whenever possible: (CAPR 110-3 §7.5.4)

No more than six colors may be used in any emblem, with black and white included in the count. (CAPR 110-3 §7.5.2)

These adjustments ensure uniformity across the wing while preserving each unit’s history and identity.

The Rule of Tincture must be followed: colors must be placed over metals, or vice versa. Colors must not be placed over colors, nor metals over metals, except in limited, approved exceptions. (CAPR 110-3 §7.5.3)

Scrolls for group and higher commands use a white background edged with Air Force Yellow and Ultramarine Blue lettering. Squadron scrolls must have contrasting colors for readability, with matching scroll border and lettering colors different from white. (CAPR 110-3 §7.5.5)

Design Decisions:

Mottos were removed from all updated designs, as they typically express values or actions our members are expected to already embody, making them redundant on the emblem.

Where numbers or text would traditionally appear inside the emblem, stars were used instead for a consistent visual element.

For units without a current emblem or whose designs lacked specific elements, references to older emblems were incorporated to maintain historical continuity and honor tradition.

Colors were standardized across all emblems to align with official palette and to promote consistency throughout the wing.

All references to CAP past and present branding were updated to focus on using the CAP roundel per CAPR 110-3 §7.3.14: The CAP logo may be included but is not required. The former former CAP roundel (the emblem without text and the red stripe) may be used to honor heritage.

Choosing the background color for the scroll was a challenge when deciding what to keep or remove from older emblems. I found that keeping the gold border looks good, but we cannot use blue text like on shields.

All scrolls were aligned at a 120-degree angle and given a yellow border matching the shield borders to ensure uniformity. The other two options, 90 and 150 degrees, made the emblem appear unbalanced or added excessive dead space to the scrolls.

Guidance for emblem typography and scroll layout follows DAFI 84-105 (as detailed in Guide to Air Force Heraldry).

On the scroll(s) of a disc‑style emblem: use all‑caps lettering; in software such as CorelDraw, use Switzerland Black Condensed, or for Microsoft products use Arial Black, 36 pt — except “ST,” “TH,” and “D” at the end of numbers, which should be 25 pt. The scroll may arc to 90°, 120°, or 150°, depending on space; both scrolls must use the same arc length with borders for quality manufacture. The border of the disc and scroll(s) must match the lettering color.
