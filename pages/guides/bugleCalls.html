Bugle Calls

Bugle calls are used to communicate commands and signal activities during Civil Air Patrol events. Some calls are not observed in CAP but are part of military tradition.

Bugle calls are used to communicate commands and signal activities in military and CAP tradition.

Bugle Call Description Duration
Adjutant's Call Indicates that the adjutant is about to form the formation.
Assembly Signals troops to assemble at a designated place.
Attention Sounded as a warning that troops are about to be called to attention.
Boots and Saddles Sounded for mounted troops to mount and take their place in line.
Call to Quarters Signals all personnel not authorized to be absent to return to their quarters for the night.
Church Call Signals that religious services are about to begin; may announce formation of a funeral escort.
Drill Call Sounds as a warning to turn out for drill.
Fatigue Call Signals all designated personnel to report for fatigue duty.
Fire Call Signals a fire on post or in the vicinity; also used for fire drills.
First Call Sounds as a warning that personnel will prepare to assemble for formation.
First Sergeant's Call Signals that the First Sergeant is about to form the formation.
Mail Call Signals personnel to assemble for the distribution of mail.
Mess Call Signals mealtime.
Officers Call Signals all officers to assemble at a designated place.
Pay Call Signals that troops will be paid.
Recall Signals duties or drills to cease.
Retreat Signals the end of the official day. Played before "To The Colors".
Reveille Signals troops to awaken for morning roll call; accompanies raising of the flag.
The Rouse Used in Commonwealth nations to signal soldiers to get out of bed.
School Call Signals school is about to begin.
Sick Call Signals all troops needing medical attention to report to the dispensary.
Taps Signals that unauthorized lights are to be extinguished; also sounded at military funerals.
Tattoo Signals lights out and that all loud talking and disturbances be discontinued within 15 minutes.
To The Colors Used to render honors to the nation. Played when no band is available, immediately following Retreat.
To Arms Signals all troops to fall under arms at designated places without delay.

History of Bugle Calls

Every evening at 1700, soldiers, family members, and civilian employees honor the nation while continuing a tradition as old as the US Army. Cars stop, work pauses, and everyone renders honors to the flag.

George Washington first assumed command of the Continental Army in 1775. Drums and bugles were already a fixture of military life. They allowed commanders to communicate orders amidst firing muskets, clashing sabers, and booming cannons. Frontier outposts used bugle calls to coordinate tasks like gathering forage or returning from work details.

Days began early, with soldiers feeding and grooming horses before dawn. Following Reveille, breakfast was served, and Assembly signaled inspections. The Soldier with the best uniform often served as the commander's orderly.

Afternoons were spent on fatigue duties, returning to camp when Recall was sounded, leading to evening parade and Retreat. Frontier soldiers attended final parades, and prior to the 1890s, Tattoo signaled lights out at 2200, along with Sunday inspections.

Based on U.S. Army Training Center, Fort Jackson Bugle Calls.
